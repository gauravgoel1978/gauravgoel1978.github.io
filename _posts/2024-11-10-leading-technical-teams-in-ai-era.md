After 21+ years in the industry — managing engineers, delivering multi-million dollar programs, and now actively deploying Generative AI in our workflows — I've developed a perspective on what technical leadership looks like in 2025. Spoiler: it's more human than ever.

## The Myth of AI Replacing Engineers

Every six months, a new headline claims AI will replace software engineers. I've seen similar fears around automation, cloud, and DevOps. The reality? These tools redefined the *type* of work engineers do, not whether engineers are needed.

AI amplifies the capable and exposes the underprepared. The engineers on my team who've thrived are those who:

1. **Treat AI as a tool, not a crutch** — they understand what the model does and doesn't know
2. **Ask better questions** — prompt engineering is, at its core, structured thinking
3. **Review AI outputs critically** — they trust but verify, catching hallucinations before they reach production

## What's Actually Changed

### Velocity
With AI-assisted code review, test generation, and debugging, a skilled engineer today can cover ground that would have taken a team of three just five years ago. This raises the bar for what "good output" looks like.

### The Feedback Loop
AI tools compress the feedback loop dramatically. A question that previously meant a 2-hour Slack thread or a JTAC ticket can now be answered in seconds — if you have the right context engineering in place.

### Documentation
LLMs are extraordinary documentation consumers. Teams that maintain clean, well-structured internal documentation see dramatically better AI outputs than teams with scattered wikis and tribal knowledge.

## What Hasn't Changed

### Trust is built in person (or async, but intentionally)
I still have 1:1s. I still make time to understand what blocks each engineer. No AI tool will tell me that someone is quietly burning out, or that a team member has ideas they're not sharing in group settings.

### Architectural judgement
Knowing *why* a design is correct — not just that it works — remains irreplaceably human. I've seen AI suggest architectures that would catastrophically fail at scale, plausibly explained. Domain expertise is the guard rail.

### Escalation ownership
When a $28M customer program is at risk at 2am, it's not an AI that makes the call on whether to failback the deployment. Leadership under pressure is still leadership.

## Practical Advice for Engineering Leaders

- **Run AI hackathons.** Give your team a day to experiment with GenAI tools on real problems. The enthusiasm that results is worth more than a formal training programme.
- **Create a prompt library.** Standardise your best prompts for debugging, code review, and documentation. Treat them like code — version-controlled and reviewed.
- **Measure outcomes, not activity.** AI might mean engineers write fewer lines of code. If the outcomes are better, that's the win.
